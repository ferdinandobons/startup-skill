# Intake Brief: Trusted Mechanic Finder

## Phase 1: Intake Interview

---

### Interview Round 1: Understanding the Problem and the Idea

The user came in with the following raw input:

> "I have an idea for an app that helps people find local mechanics they can trust. Like a mix between Yelp and Angi but specifically for auto repair. I've been ripped off twice and I know I'm not the only one."

This is a classic founder-as-customer scenario -- personal pain driving the idea. There's a clear emotional trigger (being ripped off), a general concept (trustworthy mechanic discovery), and a rough positioning reference (Yelp meets Angi for auto repair). But almost everything else is undefined. Here are the questions I would ask in the first round:

**Questions (Round 1):**

1. When you say you were "ripped off," what specifically happened? Were you overcharged, given unnecessary repairs, had shoddy work done, or something else? Understanding the exact nature of the trust breakdown matters for how we design the solution.

2. When you picture this app working perfectly, what does a user actually do? Walk me through the experience -- someone's car breaks down, they open the app, and then what happens?

3. You mentioned Yelp and Angi as reference points. What do you think those platforms get wrong when it comes to auto repair specifically? What would you borrow from them, and what would you throw out?

4. Have you done anything with this idea yet -- talked to other car owners, sketched out screens, looked into what already exists in this space? Or is this still at the "shower thought" stage?

---

### Interview Round 2: The Founder and the Market

Based on the limited information provided, I would ask these follow-up questions to understand who is building this and for whom:

**Questions (Round 2):**

1. Tell me about your background. Are you technical (can build the app yourself), or would you need to hire/partner with developers? Any experience in the automotive industry?

2. Is this a full-time pursuit or a side project? What kind of budget or runway are you working with -- bootstrapping with savings, looking to raise money, or just exploring for now?

3. Who do you picture as the core user? Everyone with a car, or a more specific group -- like people new to an area who don't have a trusted mechanic yet, or maybe people who own older cars that need frequent repairs?

4. What geography are you thinking about launching in? Your local city first, a broader metro area, or nationwide from day one?

5. Do you have co-founders, or are you solo on this?

---

### Interview Round 3: Business Model and Constraints

**Questions (Round 3):**

1. How do you imagine making money? Would mechanics pay to be listed (like Angi), would users pay for a premium experience, would you take a cut of bookings, or something else entirely?

2. What does "trust" actually mean in your version of this product? Is it verified reviews, background checks on shops, price transparency, guarantees on work, or some combination?

3. What does success look like for you in 6 months? Is it a live app with users, a validated concept with pre-signups, or getting funded?

4. What are you most worried about? The biggest thing that might make this not work?

5. Any strong feelings about the brand -- should it feel premium and authoritative, or scrappy and community-driven? Should it be app-only, or also web?

---

### Summary and Confirmation Round

After gathering answers, I would summarize back to the user for confirmation:

> "Here's what I'm hearing: You want to build a mobile app that helps car owners find trustworthy local mechanics, differentiated from Yelp/Angi by being auto-repair-specific with a stronger trust layer. The idea comes from personal experience getting ripped off. You're at the very early idea stage with no prototype or validation yet. Let me know what I got right and what I missed, and we'll move forward."

- **Origin:** Personal pain. The founder has been ripped off twice and believes the problem is widespread.

## Key Observations

1. **This idea is extremely vague.** The founder has a problem and a one-sentence solution concept, but virtually no details about execution, business model, target market specificity, or competitive awareness. This is normal for an early-stage idea and exactly what the intake process is designed to flesh out.

2. **The trust mechanism is the core differentiator** -- but it's undefined. How trust is established, verified, and communicated to users will make or break this concept. Is it curated reviews? Mechanic certifications? Price transparency? Work guarantees? This needs to be explored deeply in the brainstorm phase.

3. **The competitive landscape is crowded.** Yelp, Google, and several auto-specific platforms (RepairPal, OpenBay, CarAdvise) already exist. The founder may not be aware of all of them. Market research will need to identify what gap, if any, remains.

4. **Founder-market fit signal is moderate.** Being a frustrated car owner gives authentic empathy for the problem, but there's no indication of automotive industry knowledge or technical ability to build the product. This is a risk to explore.

5. **Two-sided marketplace dynamics apply.** The app needs both mechanics and car owners. Cold-start / chicken-and-egg problems will be a major strategic consideration.
